UX Research Product Design Mobile

From "Who Owes What?" to Total Clarity

Designing a low-friction expense coordination system that makes shared living financial — and social — harmony effortless.

Role

UX Designer

Timeline

2 Sprints

Platform

iOS Mobile

Year

2024

Context

Money Doesn't Break Friendships. Unclear Money Does.

In shared households, small expenses often turn into coordination problems. Who paid? Who still owes? Did someone already buy detergent?

In interviews with 24 young adults who have lived with roommates, most described shared expense coordination as stressful and awkward.

The problem was not splitting bills. It was maintaining clear financial visibility across multiple housemates without creating friction.

The Problem

What Breaks in Shared Living

Our interviews revealed that the frustration was rarely about the amount of money — it was about the coordination. Three behavioural patterns consistently emerged:

  • 01

    Visibility gaps — Housemates often did not know who had paid for what, or whether a purchase had already been made.

  • 02

    Responsibility ambiguity — Shared expenses were loosely tracked, leading to repeated reminders and uncomfortable follow-ups.

  • 03

    Mental load accumulation — Small purchases like detergent or paper towels created ongoing cognitive overhead. Someone always had to remember.

The breakdown didn't happen at the moment of payment. It happened in the lack of shared clarity.

Design Principles

Four Guiding Priorities

To address the behavioural breakdowns uncovered in research, we defined four guiding principles before touching any screens:

  • 01

    Make responsibility visible — every shared expense should have clear ownership and status.

  • 02

    Reduce coordination effort — tracking should require minimal input and minimal follow-up.

  • 03

    Lower social friction — the system should reduce the need for direct confrontation or repeated reminders.

  • 04

    Reframe the emotional experience — shared expense management should feel collaborative and reassuring, not tense or awkward.

Design Process

From Messy Household Problem to Clear System

We didn't start by designing screens. We started by isolating the real coordination breakdowns in shared living, then validating a system that could reduce them with minimal effort.

1. Target User & Persona

We designed for housemates who split frequent everyday costs but don't want the overhead of tracking, following up, or renegotiating later. This persona clarified the behavioural priorities that guided our system design: visibility, minimal effort, and reduced social friction.

2. User Journey Mapping

The journey map revealed that friction did not stem from calculation, but from coordination breakdowns. Three recurring tension points stood out:

  • —

    Duplicate purchases due to visibility gaps — housemates were unsure what had already been bought.

  • —

    Ambiguity during expense logging — unclear ownership and split methods led to future misunderstandings.

  • —

    Discomfort when following up on payments — manual reminders created social tension.

This confirmed that we were not designing a calculator, but a coordination system.

3. Competitive Audit & Information Architecture

We analysed leading bill-splitting tools and found three consistent patterns:

  • —

    Transaction-centric design — most tools focus on calculating balances and settling payments, with limited support for coordination before or after.

  • —

    Minimal shared visibility — few platforms surface unsettled balances or recurring household needs in a way that reduces cognitive load.

  • —

    Separation between planning and logging — existing tools treat purchases and expense recording as separate flows, increasing repetitive effort.

Rather than building a more complex calculator, we focused on designing a coordination layer: pre-purchase visibility, structured logging, neutral reminders, and recurring purchase handling.

4. Wireframes & Iteration

We dedicated a full week to iterating on wireframes, stress-testing the core experience before moving into visual design. Rather than moving quickly, we focused on structural clarity and asked:

  • —

    What information must be immediately visible on the Home screen?

  • —

    How much detail is necessary before it becomes overwhelming?

  • —

    How can unsettled balances and reminders remain visible without clutter?

Early iterations included additional summaries and visual elements. Testing revealed excessive information reduced scannability. We simplified to prioritise clear debt visibility and unsettled status at a glance.

5. Design System

After validating the core structure, we developed a lightweight design system to reinforce clarity and emotional ease. The system prioritised:

  • —

    Clear visual hierarchy to emphasise balances and settlement status

  • —

    Consistent colour cues to signal payment states — green selected to create a reassuring atmosphere

  • —

    A calm, friendly tone with subtle illustrations to soften the experience without distracting from core functionality

The Solution

Make Shared Living Feel Effortless

House Fund transforms shared expense tracking from a source of tension into a predictable coordination system. Instead of chasing payments or second-guessing purchases, housemates gain clarity, structure, and peace of mind.

1. From "Who Owes What?" to Instant Clarity

The Home dashboard makes balances and transaction details instantly visible. Users can tap a Remind button, but recipients receive a neutral system-generated notification rather than a personal message — perceived as far less confrontational in testing.

2. From Scattered Receipts to Structured Coordination

When logging an expense, users define ownership, participants, split method, and settlement timing upfront. Unsettled items remain clearly visible on the Home dashboard, preventing ambiguity from accumulating. Participants described this structure as "fair" and "clear."

3. From Repetition to Effortless Routine

Recurring household items are managed through a shared To-Buy list that connects planning with expense logging. Completed items can be converted directly into structured expense entries, preventing duplicate purchases and repetitive input. Participants described this as reducing "small but constant coordination effort."

4. From Isolated Transactions to Shared Financial Transparency

The Tracker provides a monthly overview of shared and individual spending across categories. Users can instantly see total household spending without reviewing transactions one by one. Participants described this as time-saving and helpful for personal budgeting.

Iteration

Usability Test & Key Changes

1. Improving Visual Clarity & Information Hierarchy

Usability testing revealed that dense information and low colour contrast made key insights harder to identify at a glance. We improved visual hierarchy and category differentiation to reduce cognitive load and enhance clarity.

2. Reducing Decision Friction in Split Selection

Usage data showed uneven adoption across split methods. We reprioritised options based on real behaviour, reducing friction during expense entry and surfacing the most-used options first.

Outcomes

Coordination Made Effortless

HouseFund transformed shared expense management from a recurring source of social friction into a lightweight coordination habit.

  • —

    Reduced uncertainty — real-time balance visibility eliminated ambiguity around who owed what

  • —

    Fewer awkward check-ins — neutral system reminders replaced uncomfortable personal follow-ups

  • —

    Lower cognitive load — the To-Buy list and recurring expense handling reduced the mental overhead of shared living

  • —

    Greater financial clarity — the Tracker simplified personal budgeting without adding extra effort
